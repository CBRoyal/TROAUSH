U.S.

Highways

U.S. Highways are integrated routes and highways numbered with a nationwide grid in the contiguous U.S. They're sometimes called federal highways, as the designation and numbering of these highways were coordinated among the U.S., but the roadways were built and are currently maintained by state or local governments since 1926. Unlike later Interstate highways, U.S. highways don't have a minimum design standard, as they're not usually built to freeway standards. Many are designed as main streets of certain cities and towns they run through and very few are designed as toll roads, except they don't have any tool bridges or tunnels. This webpage takes you on a road trip down all of the facts about the U.S. highways in America.

Numbering System

Much like the Interstate highways, the U.S. highways follow a grid numbering pattern, in which routes assigned odd-numbers run north-south and routes assigned even-numbers run east-west, though routes assigned three-digit numbers can be either-or. However, some two-digit routes don't run in their general direction. Unlike the Interstate Highway Numbering System, the U.S. Highway Numbering System assigns lower odd numbers in the east, higher odd numbers in the west, lower even numbers in the north, and higher even numbers in the south. The one- and two-digit U.S. highways are major routes, while three-digit U.S. highways are shorter spur routes from a main U.S. highway and just like Interstate highways, not all of them run in the same direction as their parent U.S. highways.

Signage

U.S. Highways are typically displayed as large black rectangles with white shields displayed within them and their distinct route number displayed within the shield. Individual states around America manufactures their own U.S. highway signs, as they may use cut-out or rectangular designs, outline black outlines, and print the letters "US" above the numerals. One- and two-digit numbered shields feature the same large, bold numerals on a square-dimension shield, while 3-digit routes may either use the same shield with a narrow font, or a wider rectangular-dimension shield. Special routes may be indicated with a banner above the route number, or with a letter suffixed to the route number.

Divided U.S. Highways

Some U.S. highways are divided into two equivalent separate routes of the main highway and then rejoined in another location. However, only one of US-6's two routes in Pennsylvania, US-6N doesn't rejoin US-6 as its west end. Special routes are assigned lettered suffixes, like A for alternate and B for business.

History of the U.S. Highways

U.S highways were originally called auto trails from 1903-1926. Auto trail associations made the system of road marking haphazardous and ununiformed. In 1925, the Joint Board on Interstate Highways worked to form a national numbering system to rationalize the roads. After several meetings, a final report was approved by the U.S. Department of Agriculture in November, 1925. The U.S. Highway system was approved on November 11, 1926.

Expansion

Expansion of the U.S. Highway System continued until 1956, when the Interstate Highway System was developed under the administration of Dwight D. Eisenhower. After the national implementation of the Interstate Highway system, many U.S. highways bypassed or overlaid by or with Interstate highways have been removed from the system. In some places, where there are no nearby Interstate highways, the U.S. highways often remain as the most well-developed roads for long-distance travel. While the system's growth has slowed in recent decades, the U.S. Highway System remains in place to this day and new are are added to the system.
